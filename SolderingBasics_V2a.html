Iron care & tinning01

Keep the tip wet, shiny, and silver.

A clean, tinned tip transfers heat efficiently. A dry, oxidized tip won't — and you'll fight every joint until it's restored.

Heat up

Let the iron reach temperature (~350 °C / 660 °F for leaded solder).

Wipe

Clean on a damp sponge or brass wool — brass preferred, it doesn't shock the tip.

Tin

Melt a small bead of solder onto the tip until it coats silver.

Re-tin often

Between joints and before setting the iron back in its stand.

Power off tinned

Always leave a fresh layer of solder on the tip when you finish.

Joint anatomy02

What a good joint looks like.

✓ Good — concave fillet

Smooth, shiny, concave slope from lead to pad. Solder has wetted both surfaces.

✗ Cold / dry joint

Dull, lumpy, or convex blob. Often gaps at the lead — solder didn't wet properly.

Through-hole technique03

Heat the joint, not the solder.

Note: in normal through-hole soldering we bend the leads slightly to keep the component in place, but we don't do this for header pins — the leads should stay straight.

Seat the part

Insert the header pins through the holes. Keep the strip flat against the board — don't bend the leads.

Heat both

Touch the iron to the pad and the lead at once. Hold ~2 seconds.

Feed solder

Apply solder to the joint — not the iron tip. It should flow in.

Pull solder, then iron

Remove solder first, leave the iron a half-second longer, then lift.

Dos & Don'ts04

Habits that separate clean joints from rework.

  • DOPark the iron in its stand between joints — never on the bench.
  • DOTin a fresh tip before the first joint of every session.
  • DOLet joints cool naturally; don't blow on them.
  • DOUse just enough solder to form a clean fillet — more isn't better.
  • DOKeep the iron tip clean: wipe before every joint.
  • DON'TTouch the metal shaft, or set the iron on anything but its stand.
  • DON'TMelt solder onto the tip and carry it to the joint — it won't wet.
  • DON'THold the iron on the joint for more than ~3 seconds — pads lift.
  • DON'TFile or sand the tip when it gets dirty — re-tin instead.
  • DON'TEat, drink, or rub your eyes at the bench.
Cleanup05

Finish the job.

  1. Wait for the joint to fully cool and solidify before touching.
  2. Clean flux residue from the PCB with isopropyl alcohol (IPA) and cotton swabs.
  3. Re-tin the iron tip, then power down and return it to its stand.
  4. Wash your hands thoroughly with soap and water — we are using leaded solder, so do this before eating, drinking, or touching your face.
Safety06

The non-negotiables.

Fumes

A fume evacuation fan will be provided at your workstation — keep it positioned close enough to the joint so the smoke pulls away from your face.

Burns

The iron tip and shaft reach ~350 °C and stay dangerously hot for a long time after powering down. Treat them as hot until they're cool to the touch — and don't test that with your fingers.

Hands

Thoroughly wash hands with soap and water after the session — we're using leaded solder.

Common mistakes & fixes07

If something looks off, it probably is.

Dull, gray joint

Cold joint — part moved while cooling, or iron wasn't hot enough. Reheat until it flows shiny.

Solder beads up

Surfaces aren't hot or clean. Re-tin the tip, heat both pad and lead longer.

Pad lifted

Iron held too long. Stop, let it cool, and ask for help — don't keep heating.

Bridge between pads

Too much solder. Remove with desoldering braid, then redo the joint.
